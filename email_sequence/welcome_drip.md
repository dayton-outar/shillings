**Day 2 – Education on Ratios**

**Subject:** P/E, P/B, and Dividends – What the Numbers Really Mean

**Hi [First Name],**

Yesterday we introduced the idea that numbers tell a story. Today let’s look at the most common ratios used by Jamaican analysts – and what they reveal about a company.

---

### Price‑to‑Earnings (P/E) Ratio

**Formula:** Share Price ÷ Earnings Per Share

**Example:** Seprod (SEP) has a P/E of 13.46x. That means investors pay about $13.46 for every $1 of profit.

**What it tells you:**
- **High P/E** → Market expects future growth (or stock may be overvalued)
- **Low P/E** → Stock may be undervalued or the company may have weak growth prospects

---

### Price‑to‑Book (P/B) Ratio

**Formula:** Market Price per Share ÷ Book Value per Share

**Example:** TransJamaican Highway (TJH) has a P/B of 6.46x. The market values it well above its accounting net worth because its toll roads generate stable, long-term cash flows.

**What it tells you:**
- P/B ≈ 1 → Market value close to accounting value
- Very high P/B → Strong expected future returns (common for infrastructure and growth stocks)

---

### Dividend Yield

**Formula:** (Annual Dividend per Share ÷ Share Price) × 100%

**Example:** TJH’s dividend yield is 3.73%. That means for every $100 you invest, you get $3.73 back each year in dividends.

**What it tells you:**
- Higher yield → More income, but sometimes signals risk (e.g., a falling share price)
- Lower yield → Company reinvests profits for growth

These are the three most common metrics. Master them and you’ll already be ahead of most retail investors.

**Day 3 – Portfolio Construction**

**Subject:** Putting the Pieces Together – How to Build Your Portfolio

**Hi [First Name],**

So you know what P/E, P/B and dividend yield mean. Now the real question: *How do you combine stocks to create a portfolio that suits your goals?*

Here’s a simple framework we use with our clients in Jamaica:

### Step 1: Define Your Goal
- **Income‑focused** – you want regular dividends (e.g., TJH, JMMB)
- **Growth‑focused** – you want price appreciation (e.g., SEP, CCC)
- **Balanced** – a mix of both

### Step 2: Diversify Across Sectors
Don’t put all your money in one industry. A good Jamaican starter portfolio might include:
- **Consumer goods** (e.g., Seprod)
- **Infrastructure** (e.g., TransJamaican Highway)
- **Manufacturing** (e.g., Caribbean Cement)
- **Financials** (e.g., JMMB, NCB)

### Step 3: Use Ratios as Filters
- **P/E < 15** for value plays; **P/E > 20** for growth – but only if earnings are growing
- **P/B < 2** for asset‑heavy companies; higher is okay for strong cash‑flow businesses
- **Dividend yield > 2%** for income; check payout ratio

### Step 4: Allocate by Risk Tolerance
- **Conservative:** 60% bonds/fixed income, 30% dividend stocks, 10% growth stocks
- **Moderate:** 40% fixed income, 40% balanced stocks, 20% growth stocks
- **Aggressive:** 20% fixed income, 30% balanced, 50% growth
