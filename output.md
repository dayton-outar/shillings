# How to Read an Equity Research Recommendation Sheet

This guide explains the key metrics and concepts found on a typical equity research recommendation sheet, using examples from stocks on the Jamaica Stock Exchange (JSE). These metrics help investors evaluate a company’s valuation, income potential, and overall quality before making portfolio decisions.

---

## 1. Stock Ticker

A ticker is the short trading symbol assigned to a company on a stock exchange. It is used to identify the stock quickly when placing trades or reading market data.

**Examples:**
- **SEP** – Seprod Limited
- **TJH** – TransJamaican Highway Limited
- **CCC** – Caribbean Cement Company Limited

---

## 2. Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio

**Example:** SEP P/E = 13.46x

**Formula:**

\[
\text{P/E} = \frac{\text{Share Price}}{\text{Earnings Per Share (EPS)}}
\]

This ratio tells you how much investors are willing to pay for each dollar of a company’s profit.

### Interpretation
- **High P/E** – Investors expect strong future growth, or the stock may be overvalued.
- **Low P/E** – The stock may be undervalued, or the business may have weak growth prospects.

**Example:** CCC at 12.93x means investors are paying about 13 times the company’s annual earnings.

---

## 3. Price-to-Book (P/B) Ratio

**Example:** TJH P/B = 6.46x

**Formula:**

\[
\text{P/B} = \frac{\text{Market Price per Share}}{\text{Book Value per Share}}
\]

Book value is the accounting net worth of the company (assets minus liabilities).

### Interpretation
- **P/B around 1** – The market values the company close to its accounting net worth.
- **Very high P/B** – Investors believe the company can generate strong future returns above its book value.

TJH has a high P/B because its infrastructure assets (toll roads) can generate stable, long-term cash flows.

---

## 4. Share Price

**Example:** SEP price = $81.73

This is simply the current market price at which one share of the stock is trading.

---

## 5. Dividend Yield

**Example:** TJH dividend yield = 3.73%

**Formula:**

\[
\text{Dividend Yield} = \frac{\text{Annual Dividend per Share}}{\text{Share Price}} \times 100\%
\]

This measures the income return an investor receives from dividends, expressed as a percentage of the share price.

### Example
If a stock pays an annual dividend of $3.00 per share and its price is $80.00, the dividend yield is:

\[
\frac{3.00}{80.00} \times 100\% = 3.75\%
\]

A higher yield may indicate a good income stock, but it can also be a red flag if the dividend is unsustainable.

---

## 6. Using These Metrics to Structure a Portfolio

A wealth management newsletter recently advised investors to combine these metrics to build a balanced portfolio:

- **Growth stocks** (higher P/E, low or no dividend) – for capital appreciation.
- **Value stocks** (low P/E, low P/B) – for undervalued opportunities.
- **Income stocks** (high dividend yield, stable earnings) – for regular income.
- **Defensive stocks** (low P/E, moderate yield, strong qualitative factors) – for downside protection.

By analyzing each stock through these lenses, you can decide how much to allocate to each category based on your risk tolerance and investment goals.
