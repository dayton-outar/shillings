Ever seen a one-page equity research sheet that breaks down stocks with clear metrics (P/E, P/B, dividend yield, and weight recommendations)? A Jamaican wealth management firm recently published one for the JSE. Here’s what each metric means and how you can build a similar system for your portfolio. 🧵

Tweet 2:
First, the basics: a stock ticker (e.g., SEP = Seprod) and share price ($81.73) are the entry point. Tickers are the exchange’s shorthand; share price is the current market cost of one share. Simple, but essential for all comparisons.

Tweet 3:
P/E (Price-to-Earnings) = share price ÷ earnings per share. At 13.46x, investors pay $13.46 for every $1 of Seprod’s profit. High P/E suggests growth expectations; low P/E may indicate a bargain or weak prospects. Compare across similar industries.

Tweet 4:
P/B (Price-to-Book) = market price ÷ book value per share. Book value is accounting net worth. TJH at 6.46x means the market values it far above its accounting net worth—common for infrastructure firms with stable cash flows. P/B near 1 = priced at book.

Tweet 5:
Dividend Yield = annual dividend per share ÷ share price × 100%. TJH pays 3.73%, meaning $3.73 per $100 invested. A key metric for income investors. High yield can signal a strong cash return, but always check payout sustainability.

Tweet 6:
Weight recommendation tells you how much of your portfolio to allocate to a stock. “Overweight” = hold more than the index weight (bullish). “Market weight” = neutral. “Underweight” = hold less (bearish). It’s the action step from the analysis.

Tweet 7:
Beyond numbers, the sheet includes qualitative assessment: industry outlook, competitive position, and management quality. Numbers tell you what happened; story tells you what might happen. A balanced analysis combines both.

Tweet 8:
Putting it together: Use P/E and P/B for valuation, dividend yield for income, and weight recommendations for portfolio structure. That’s the core of smart asset allocation—no more guessing, just data-driven decisions.
