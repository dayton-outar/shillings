# The 4 Ratios Every Portfolio Manager Checks Before Allocating

I came across a fascinating piece from a local wealth management firm that broke down exactly how they evaluate stocks before adding them to a portfolio. It’s not magic — it’s four simple ratios that separate disciplined allocation from guesswork.

Whether you're managing a family trust or your own retirement account, these are the numbers that matter. Let’s walk through them with real examples from the Jamaica Stock Exchange (JSE).

---

## 1. P/E Ratio (Price-to-Earnings)

**Example:** Seprod Limited (SEP) at 13.46x

**Formula:**  
\[
P/E = \frac{\text{Share Price}}{\text{Earnings Per Share}}
\]

Portfolio managers check this first. It tells you how much you’re paying for every dollar of earnings.

- **High P/E** → Investors expect strong future growth (or the stock is overvalued).  
- **Low P/E** → Possibly undervalued, but could signal weak prospects.

A P/E of 13x means you’re paying $13 for each $1 of this year’s earnings. That’s reasonable for a quality company like Seprod.

## 2. P/B Ratio (Price-to-Book)

**Example:** TransJamaican Highway (TJH) at 6.46x

**Formula:**  
\[
P/B = \frac{\text{Market Price per Share}}{\text{Book Value per Share}}
\]

Book value is the accounting net worth of the company. The P/B ratio tells you whether the market values the company above or below its tangible assets.

- **P/B = 1** → Market price matches the company’s net assets.
- **P/B > 3** → Investors believe the company can generate returns far above its asset base.

TJH’s high P/B makes sense: toll roads produce stable, long-term cash flows that are worth a premium.

## 3. Dividend Yield

**Example:** TransJamaican Highway (TJH) at 3.73%

**Formula:**  
\[
\text{Dividend Yield} = \frac{\text{Annual Dividend per Share}}{\text{Share Price}} \times 100\%
\]

Income-focused managers live and die by this number. It measures the cash return you get from dividends alone.

A 3.73% yield on TJH means that for every $100 invested, you receive $3.73 annually in dividends — before any capital gains. For retirees or trust portfolios, that’s often the baseline.

## 4. Debt-to-Equity Ratio

**Example:** Caribbean Cement (CCC) at 0.45x (hypothetical from industry norms)

**Formula:**  
\[
\text{D/E} = \frac{\text{Total Liabilities}}{\text{Shareholders’ Equity}}
\]

Portfolio managers check how much leverage a company uses. High debt amplifies returns in good times but increases risk in downturns.

- **D/E < 1** → More equity than debt – conservative.  
- **D/E > 2** → Highly leveraged – risky but possibly high growth.

Mature industries like cement tend to keep D/E below 0.5. Knowing this helps you avoid companies that are one recession away from trouble.

---

## Why These Four?

Because together they cover the three pillars of portfolio allocation:

- **Valuation** – P/E and P/B tell you if you’re overpaying.  
- **Income** – Dividend yield shows what you’ll actually earn in cash.  
- **Risk** – Debt-to-equity signals financial stability.
