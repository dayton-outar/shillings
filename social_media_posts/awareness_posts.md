### Post 1: What is P/E Ratio and Why Does It Matter?

**Headline:** Understanding P/E Ratio – The Price You Pay for Every Dollar of Profit

If you’re investing on the Jamaica Stock Exchange, “P/E” is one of the first numbers you’ll see. Let’s break it down with a real JSE example.

**What is P/E?**  
Price-to-Earnings ratio = Share Price ÷ Earnings Per Share. It tells you how many years of current earnings it would take to recover your investment (if earnings remained constant).

**Real JSE example: Seprod Limited (SEP)**  
- Share Price: $81.73  
- P/E: 13.46x  

This means investors are paying $13.46 for every $1 of Seprod’s annual profit. A P/E of 13x is considered moderate – neither excessively cheap nor expensive.

**What does a high/low P/E mean?**  
- **Low P/E (<10)** : Possibly undervalued, or market expects weak growth.  
- **High P/E (>20)** : Market expects strong future growth – or stock may be overvalued.

For context, Caribbean Cement Company (CCC) trades at a P/E of 12.93x, similar to Seprod. Both are in a reasonable range for JSE blue chips.

**Why this matters:**  
P/E helps you compare companies within the same sector and decide if a stock is fairly priced. But never use P/E alone – combine it with growth prospects and debt levels.

### Post 2: Dividend Yield – The Income Your Portfolio Actually Pays You

**Headline:** Dividend Yield 101 – How Much Cash Does Your Stock Put in Your Pocket?

Dividends are a powerful part of total return, especially in a market like Jamaica where many companies pay consistent dividends.

**Formula:**  
Dividend Yield = (Annual Dividend per Share ÷ Share Price) × 100%

**Real JSE example: TransJamaican Highway (TJH)**  
- Share Price: ≈ $35.00 (illustrative)  
- Annual Dividend per Share: $1.30 (approx)  
- Dividend Yield: 3.73%

That means for every $100 invested in TJH, you receive $3.73 annually in cash (before taxes).

**How to interpret dividend yield:**  
- **Higher yield (>5%)** : More income, but sometimes a red flag if the dividend is unsustainable.  
- **Lower yield (<2%)** : Company may be reinvesting profits for growth (common in tech or high-growth stocks).

**JSE context:**  
Companies like TJH (toll road infrastructure) offer stable, predictable dividends because of long-term concession contracts. Compare that to growth stocks like some junior market listings which may pay no dividend at all.

**Key insight:**  
Don’t chase yield blindly. A high yield could mean the stock price has fallen sharply, making the yield appear attractive but the business may be in trouble. Always check the payout ratio and earnings stability.

### Post 3: Overweight vs Marketweight – The Language of Portfolio Allocation

**Headline:** Overweight, Marketweight, Underweight – What Do They Mean for Your JSE Portfolio?

If you’ve ever seen an analyst report, you’ve probably come across these terms. They describe how much of a stock you should hold relative to its weight in a benchmark index.

**Simple definitions:**  
- **Marketweight** : Hold the same percentage as the stock’s weight in the JSE Index. For example, if Seprod is 5% of the JSE Index, you should have 5% of your portfolio in SEP.  
- **Overweight** : Hold more than the index weight – you expect that stock to outperform.  
- **Underweight** : Hold less – you expect it to underperform.

**Real JSE example:**  
Suppose the JSE Combined Index has these components (simplified):
- Seprod: 6% weight  
- TransJamaican Highway: 4% weight  
- Caribbean Cement: 3% weight

If our research says TJH will benefit from infrastructure spending, we might assign an **overweight** rating (e.g., 8% of portfolio instead of 4%). Conversely, if we see headwinds for cement, we could make CCC **underweight** (e.g., 1% instead of 3%).

**Why this matters for you:**  
- It prevents you from being overexposed to one sector.  
- It forces disciplined rebalancing.  
- It aligns your portfolio with your conviction level.

**Mistake #1: Chasing the highest P/E without understanding why.**  
A stock like CCC with P/E of 12.9x might look cheap, but if its earnings are declining, it could be a value trap. Always check earnings trend.

A 5% yield is tempting. But if the company pays out 90% of its earnings as dividends, a small drop in profit could force a cut. TJH’s 3.7% yield is backed by stable toll revenue – much safer.

**Mistake #3: Not using allocation weights.**  
Many investors buy “good stories” without considering how much of their portfolio is in one stock. If you own 15% of your portfolio in one JSE stock, you are taking unnecessary risk.

Use a simple framework:
- Start with P/E and P/B to filter cheap vs expensive.
