# Charts And Technical Analysis

Charts help investors see price history quickly. They do not prove the future, but they make patterns, volatility, drawdowns, recoveries, and market reactions easier to understand.

Technical analysis studies price movement over time. It asks what the market appears to be doing, not what a company is worth from its financial statements.

The best use of charts is not blind prediction. The best use is context.

## Reading Charts

Every chart tells a story, but the story can be misleading if the investor ignores context.

![Four charts showing NVIDIA, Bitcoin, 10-year US yield, and US dollar to euro exchange-rate movement](/.attachments/10.9-four.charts.nvidia.bonds.usd.png)

_Four charts showing NVIDIA, Bitcoin, 10-year US yield, and US dollar to euro exchange-rate movement_

Useful chart-reading questions:

- What asset is shown?
- What time period is shown?
- What happened during that period?
- Is the movement large for this asset type?
- Is the trend short-term or long-term?
- Does the movement match business results?
- Was there a catalyst?
- Is the chart showing price, return, yield, or currency movement?

Investors often interpret charts through different beliefs:

- Mean reversion: large moves eventually reverse.
- Momentum: strong trends can continue.
- Contrarian thinking: crowded views can become dangerous.

These beliefs can conflict. The same chart may tell different investors to buy, hold, or sell.

## Patterns

Chart patterns are templates traders use to interpret price behavior.

A double top is a reversal pattern. A bearish double top starts with an uptrend, forms two peaks, then breaks downward.

![Anatomy of Bearish double top pattern with resistance, neckline, and breakout point](/.attachments/10.10-bearish.double.top.png)

_Anatomy of Bearish double top pattern with resistance, neckline, and breakout point_

Patterns usually have bullish and bearish versions.

![Bullish and bearish double top reference patterns](/.attachments/10.11-bearish.bullish.double.top.png)

_Bullish and bearish double top reference patterns_

A continuation pattern pauses a trend before the original direction resumes.

![Bullish and bearish flag patterns](/.attachments/10.12-bearish.bullish.flag.pattern.png)

_Bullish and bearish flag patterns_

Important pattern concepts:

- Support: a price level where buyers may prevent further decline.
- Resistance: a price level where sellers may prevent further rise.
- Breakout: a move through support or resistance.
- Reversal: a trend changes direction.
- Continuation: a trend pauses and then continues.

Patterns are not laws. They can fail. They matter partly because many market participants watch the same signals.

The shorter the holding period, the more chart patterns may matter. Long-term investors may care less about short-term support and resistance, but they can still use charts to understand timing and market mood.

## Interpreting A Chart

Advanced chart views often combine price history with indicators.

![Example stock chart with visual indicators and price movement over time](/.attachments/10.13-nuscale.finviz.png)

_Example stock chart with visual indicators and price movement over time_

Technical traders may look for:

- Trend direction.
- Momentum.
- Support and resistance.
- Volume changes.
- Moving-average crossovers.
- Volatility expansion.
- Consolidation.
- Breakouts.

Long-term investors can use the same charts differently:

- To understand how the market reacted to catalysts.
- To avoid buying immediately after extreme excitement.
- To notice persistent weakness.
- To compare price movement with fundamentals.
- To decide whether a thesis needs review.

## Using Charts To Interpret Price Changes

Fundamental analysis asks what an asset should be worth based on business evidence. Technical analysis asks what price movement suggests about market behavior.

Neither view is complete by itself. A strong process can use both.

## Candlesticks

Candlestick charts show open, high, low, and close prices for a time period.

![Candlestick chart showing three trading days of NVIDIA](/.attachments/10.18-candlestick.nvidia.png)

_Candlestick chart showing three trading days of NVIDIA_

A bullish candle closes above its opening price. A bearish candle closes below its opening price.

![Anatomy of bullish and bearish candlesticks, including body, wick, high, low, open, and close](/.attachments/10.19-candlestick.anatomy.png)

_Anatomy of bullish and bearish candlesticks, including body, wick, high, low, open, and close_

Candlestick parts:

- Body: difference between opening and closing price.
- Upper wick: highest price reached during the period.
- Lower wick: lowest price reached during the period.
- Large body: strong momentum.
- Small body: indecision.
- Long wick: rejection of a high or low price level.

## Candlestick Patterns

Candlestick patterns help describe market behavior across one or more periods.

![Common candlestick patterns](/.attachments/10.20-various.candlestick.patterns.png)

_Common candlestick patterns_

Common patterns:

- Doji: opening and closing prices are almost the same, suggesting indecision.
- Hammer: small body with a long lower wick, often watched after a downtrend.
- Engulfing: a large candle overwhelms the previous candle, suggesting a momentum shift.
- Shooting star: price rises during the period but closes weakly.
- Spinning top: high movement but little net progress.

Pattern names make discussion easier. They do not guarantee outcomes.

## Charts Based On Averages

Moving averages smooth price data so trends are easier to see.

Simple moving average:

$\large{\text{SMA} = \frac{\text{sum of prices in the window}}{\text{number of prices in the window}}}$

Exponential moving average gives more weight to recent prices.

Weighted moving average gives linearly increasing weight to newer prices.

Hull moving average is designed to react quickly while still smoothing noise.

In general, sensitivity to recent movement can be thought of as:

$\large{\text{HMA} > \text{WMA} > \text{EMA} > \text{SMA}}$

### Multiple Averages For The Same Day

| Window | Closing | SMA | EMA | WMA | HMA |
| ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| 3 days | 66.14 | 63.55 | 64.29 | 64.33 | 67.96 |
| 20 days | 66.14 | 58.33 | 58.83 | 60.60 | 64.19 |

Interpretation:

- SMA is slower and useful for long-term trend analysis.
- EMA reacts faster and can support shorter-term strategies.
- WMA balances recent emphasis with smoothing.
- HMA is more sensitive to rapid trend changes.

## Moving Average Ribbons

Moving average ribbons show several moving averages with different windows on the same chart.

Narrow ribbons can indicate consolidation. Wide ribbons can indicate stronger trend movement.

![Moving average ribbon showing several moving averages around NVIDIA price movement](/.attachments/10.21-moving.averages.png)

_Moving average ribbon showing several moving averages around NVIDIA price movement_

Moving-average signals can help identify trend direction, but they should be tested before being used for trading rules.

## Bollinger Bands

Bollinger Bands measure volatility and help identify possible overbought or oversold conditions.

They usually include:

- Middle band: a moving average, often a 20-period SMA.
- Upper band: middle band plus a volatility range.
- Lower band: middle band minus a volatility range.

Middle band:

$\large{\text{Middle band} = \text{SMA}_{20}}$

Upper band:

$\large{\text{Upper band} = \text{SMA}_{20} + 2\sigma}$

Lower band:

$\large{\text{Lower band} = \text{SMA}_{20} - 2\sigma}$

Where $\large{\sigma}$ is the standard deviation of prices over the window.

![Bollinger Bands for NVIDIA, showing price, middle band, upper band, and lower band](/.attachments/10.22-bollinger.band.png)

_Bollinger Bands for NVIDIA_

Bands expand when volatility rises and contract when volatility falls.

Possible interpretations:

- Price crossing below the lower band may suggest a possible buy opportunity.
- Price crossing above the upper band may suggest a possible sell signal.
- Strong trends can make these signals misleading.
- A contraction after volatility can suggest consolidation before a possible breakout.

Bollinger Bands should not be used alone.

## Moving Average Convergence Divergence

Moving Average Convergence Divergence, or MACD, is a momentum indicator.

It compares short-term and long-term moving averages.

MACD line:

$\large{\text{MACD} = \text{EMA}_{12} - \text{EMA}_{26}}$

Signal line:

$\large{\text{Signal line} = \text{EMA}_{9}(\text{MACD})}$

Histogram:

$\large{\text{Histogram} = \text{MACD} - \text{Signal line}}$

![MACD chart for NVIDIA closing prices with MACD line, signal line, and histogram](/.attachments/10.23-macd.indicators.png)

_MACD chart for NVIDIA closing prices with MACD line_

Common MACD interpretations:

- Bullish signal: MACD crosses above the signal line.
- Bearish signal: MACD crosses below the signal line.
- Growing histogram: momentum is strengthening.
- Shrinking histogram: momentum may be weakening.
- Choppy markets can create false signals.

MACD is often more useful when confirmed by other indicators or fundamental evidence.

## Ichimoku Cloud

The Ichimoku Cloud combines trend, momentum, support, and resistance in one chart.

Its main elements are:

- Conversion line: short-term trend line.
- Baseline: medium-term trend line.
- Leading span A: one side of the cloud.
- Leading span B: the other side of the cloud.
- Lagging span: current price shifted backward to compare with past prices.

![Ichimoku Cloud for NVIDIA closing prices showing bullish and bearish cloud regions](/.attachments/10.24-ichimoku.png)

_Ichimoku Cloud for NVIDIA closing prices_

Common interpretations:

- Price above the cloud suggests an uptrend.
- Price below the cloud suggests a downtrend.
- Price inside the cloud suggests uncertainty or consolidation.
- A bullish signal can occur when the conversion line crosses above the baseline while price is above the cloud.
- A bearish signal can occur when the conversion line crosses below the baseline while price is below the cloud.
- A thicker cloud may suggest stronger support or resistance.

## Interactive Visualization

Interactive chart views help investors explore data without rewriting analysis each time.

Useful controls include:

- Asset selector.
- Date range.
- Chart type.
- Indicator selector.
- Comparison mode.
- Return normalization.
- Export or save view.

An investment platform should make charts readable without implying certainty. Indicators can highlight possible signals, but the user should still see uncertainty, assumptions, and data freshness.

## Using Charts Well

Charts are useful when they help the investor ask better questions:

- Did a catalyst change the price?
- Is volatility increasing?
- Is the price movement supported by fundamentals?
- Is a decline temporary or thesis-breaking?
- Is the asset behaving differently from similar assets?
- Is the investor reacting to evidence or emotion?

The durable lesson is that charts are evidence for interpretation, not proof of the future. They are powerful when combined with fundamentals, risk analysis, and disciplined review.
