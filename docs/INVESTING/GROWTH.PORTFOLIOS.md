# Growth Portfolios

Growth investing starts with a belief that something in the world is changing. A new technology may spread. A new habit may become normal. A costly problem may finally get a better solution. The investor's job is to turn that belief into a clear, testable thesis.

A growth portfolio is not just a collection of exciting companies. It is a set of positions built around researched ideas, visible risks, and measurable signals.

## Investment Thesis

An investment thesis is a researched explanation of why an investment might perform well. It should be clear enough that someone else can challenge it.

A useful thesis explains:

- What change is happening.
- Why the change matters.
- Which companies or assets may benefit.
- What evidence supports the idea.
- What would prove the idea wrong.
- When the investor should hold, sell, or keep watching.

A thesis is stronger than a theme. "Autonomous driving will be big" is a theme. "A specific group of sensor companies may rebound when demand for autonomous systems rises and funding conditions improve" is closer to a thesis.

## Starting With An Idea

The best starting point is often a field you already understand. People tend to overestimate their skill in unfamiliar areas. This is dangerous in investing because confidence can arrive before understanding.

Start with a broad idea, then keep narrowing it.

> Example:
>
> Investment idea: autonomous driving will pay off.
>
> - Robotaxis could become common.
> - Driverless trucks could reduce labor costs.
> - Autonomous vehicles need hardware, software, mapping, and infrastructure.
> - Companies that provide key components could benefit.

The idea is still too broad. It needs to be challenged.

## Challenging The Idea

Every strong thesis needs pressure. The goal is not to defend the idea emotionally. The goal is to find out whether it survives serious questions.

Useful challenge methods include:

- Strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats.
- Expert interviews.
- Opposing viewpoints.
- Ethical and security questions.
- Regulation and public acceptance.
- Competitor analysis.
- Scenarios where the technology arrives slowly or differently than expected.

> Example:
>
> Sub-thesis: ethics and security may slow autonomous driving, but may not stop it.
>
> - Accidents can create public backlash.
> - Cybersecurity matters because vehicles become connected systems.
> - Regulators may move slowly.
> - If the benefits become large enough, adoption may still continue.

Once the broad idea is challenged, divide the market into possible beneficiaries:

- Ride-hailing services.
- Car manufacturers with full automation systems.
- Truck manufacturers with automation systems.
- Appliance and platform providers.
- Component providers.

Geography also matters. A company may be strong in one country but blocked, delayed, or less relevant in another. Regulation, road design, consumer behavior, and trade policy can all affect adoption.

> Example:
>
> Sub-thesis: known brands may already be highly valued.
>
> - Large technology and automotive companies may already reflect investor excitement.
> - Smaller or less obvious companies may offer more upside.
> - Smaller companies may also carry much higher risk.

Autonomous vehicles rely on many components:

- Sensors: LiDAR, radar, cameras, and ultrasonic sensors.
- Processing units.
- Software for perception, localization, planning, decision-making, and control.
- Connectivity between vehicles and infrastructure.
- High-definition maps.
- Power supply.

This narrows the thesis toward LiDAR, a sensor technology that measures distance using light.

> Example:
>
> Refined investment idea: LiDAR may rebound.
>
> - Autonomous driving and automation still need better sensing.
> - Some LiDAR companies became cheaper after earlier market excitement faded.
> - These businesses are capital-intensive, so high interest rates can hurt them.
> - Demand may rise through robotaxis, industrial automation, robotics, mining, mapping, and smart-city uses.

## Your Investment Thesis

A practical thesis should answer three questions:

- Can this company generate much more cash in the future?
- Can it outperform competitors?
- Can it protect profits from copycats?

The last question is about an economic moat. A moat is a durable advantage that helps a company defend profits. It may come from patents, cost advantages, network effects, brand trust, regulatory position, supplier relationships, or deep technical expertise.

Margin of safety matters too. If the price already assumes everything will go perfectly, even a good company can become a poor investment.

## LiDAR Market

LiDAR is useful in autonomous driving, drones, industrial automation, mapping, architecture, engineering, geography, archaeology, and smart-city systems. The market is expected to grow, but growth in a market does not automatically mean every company in that market is a good investment.

Key questions for a LiDAR thesis include:

- Technology differentiation: which sensor design is stronger, cheaper, smaller, more durable, or more accurate?
- Cost efficiency and scalability: can the company produce useful systems at a price customers will accept?
- Strategic partnerships: has the company won relationships with automakers, robotics firms, or industrial customers?
- Regulatory environment and adoption: will safety rules, trade rules, and customer trust help or slow adoption?
- Patent portfolio: does the company own protected technology that creates an advantage?
- Competition: can small companies survive against larger suppliers?
- Financial strength and leadership: can the company fund growth without burning cash recklessly?

> Example:
>
> Patience can be an advantage. Research may end with "wait and see." If many opportunities look merely good enough, documenting the work and taking no action can be the wisest choice.

Debt analysis should include more than one ratio:

- Total debt compared with market value.
- Cash on hand.
- Cash burn rate.
- Ability to raise new capital.
- Founder ownership.
- Recent financing history.
- Layoffs or restructuring.
- Short interest and market skepticism.

A company can look troubled and still survive if it has strong backers, valuable technology, or room to raise capital. It can also look promising and still fail if funding dries up.

## Management

Leadership is especially important in young growth companies. Founder-led companies can be attractive because founders often know the product deeply and hold a long-term vision.

In the LiDAR candidate group:

- Luminar was founded by Austin Russell.
- Innoviz was co-founded and led by Omer David Keilaf.
- Aeva was co-founded and led by Soroush Salehian.
- Ouster was co-founded and led by Angus Pacala.

Founder departure can be a warning sign, especially if the founder is central to the technology, partnerships, fundraising, or market story. Leadership changes do not automatically mean trouble, but they deserve attention.

## Technology And Partnerships

Technical differences can matter deeply. A small feature that looks minor to outsiders may decide whether a product wins contracts.

The LiDAR candidates use different approaches:

- AEVA: frequency-modulated continuous wave technology.
- LAZR: hybrid solid-state technology with long-range lasers.
- INVZ: solid-state technology based on micro-electromechanical systems.
- OUST: digital solid-state technology.

Partnerships also matter because a growth company often needs large customers to prove demand.

- Luminar has worked with Volvo.
- Innoviz has supplied systems for BMW.
- Aeva has sold products to Daimler Trucks.
- Ouster has listed Zoox as a client and also targets broader uses such as smart cities.

Autonomous driving is not one single problem. A vehicle may handle parking well but struggle with unusual city streets, extreme weather, or fast highway driving. The operating context matters.

Important questions include:

- What exact use case is the company solving?
- Does the product work only in controlled settings or in messy real-world conditions?
- Is the system affordable enough for mass use?
- Can it survive weather, vibration, and long-term wear?
- Are customers testing it, buying it, or only discussing it?
- Does the company depend on one customer or one application?

## Risks

Upside analysis is exciting. Risk analysis is what keeps the investor honest.

One useful habit is to switch roles. Spend one session building the optimistic case. Spend another session trying to destroy it. If the thesis survives both moods, it is stronger.

## Falling Into Obsolescence

LiDAR may be essential for some autonomous systems, but it is not guaranteed to win every market. Camera-based approaches and improved computer vision may reduce the need for expensive laser sensors.

The risk is simple: if customers can achieve good enough performance without LiDAR, they may avoid the cost.

Questions to ask:

- What alternatives can replace or reduce LiDAR?
- Are customers choosing LiDAR because it is necessary or because it is currently convenient?
- Does the company serve markets beyond cars?
- Can its technology remain useful if automotive adoption slows?

## Squashed By Industry Giants

Small companies can innovate quickly, but large companies have capital, customers, manufacturing scale, and trust.

A startup may have a strong product and still lose if a major platform company or established supplier offers a good enough alternative. Automakers may prefer a known supplier if both products perform similarly, because supply reliability matters.

Questions to ask:

- Which larger companies already serve the market?
- Could a customer build the technology internally?
- Would customers risk depending on a small supplier?
- Does the startup offer something that large competitors cannot copy easily?

## Globalization And Conflicts

Geopolitics can affect supply chains, customer access, regulation, and investor confidence.

Risks include:

- Conflict in a company's home country.
- Trade restrictions.
- Blacklists or national-security investigations.
- State-backed competitors.
- Export controls.
- Supply-chain concentration.

A technology can be excellent and still face market barriers because of politics.

## Ongoing Analysis

Research does not end when the first thesis is written. A growth thesis needs monitoring because the evidence changes.

An investor should keep watching:

- Product progress.
- New contracts.
- Earnings reports.
- Management commentary.
- Competitor moves.
- Search trends.
- News sentiment.
- Regulatory changes.
- Customer adoption.
- Cash burn.
- Financing activity.

Sometimes the right action is to wait. If money is committed too early, the investor may miss a better opportunity later.

## Media

Media can help, but it must be read carefully.

Useful sources include:

- Investment research platforms.
- Analyst articles.
- Newsletters.
- Investor forums.
- Company announcements.
- Social media.
- Reputable newspapers.
- Specialist industry publications.
- Earnings calls and transcripts.

The main challenge is separating signal from promotion. Companies rarely describe their own quarter in blunt negative terms. Investors need to listen for what is missing, softened, delayed, or repeated too often.

Automated research assistants can help organize information, but their answers must be checked. They can miss context, invent details, or make uncertain claims sound confident.

## Measuring Success

Every thesis needs success measures. If the investor does not know what to measure, the thesis is still too vague.

For an autonomous-driving thesis, possible success measures include:

- Number of robotaxis operating in real streets.
- New customer contracts.
- Product adoption by major manufacturers.
- Successful safety milestones.
- Revenue growth from production customers.
- Lower cash burn.
- Improving margins.
- Evidence that pilots are becoming commercial deployments.

Some breakthroughs will not show up immediately in earnings. That is why the investor needs both financial and nonfinancial signals.

## What Is Next

After the LiDAR analysis, reasonable investors may reach different decisions:

- Bold investors may see enough evidence to invest.
- Cautious investors may keep researching or avoid the opportunity.
- Some investors may start with a small position and continue monitoring.

The decision depends on risk tolerance, time horizon, conviction, evidence quality, and opportunity cost.

> Example:
>
> Speculative ideas to explore:
>
> - New uses for mRNA technology, including cancer treatment.
> - Climate mitigation, renewable energy, and carbon sequestration.
> - Weight loss drugs with broad global demand.
> - Plant-based food that can compete strongly with meat on taste.
> - Technologies that address geopolitical threats.
> - Indirect beneficiaries of artificial intelligence, such as energy providers for data centers.

The durable lesson is that growth investing rewards structured curiosity. A good investor keeps asking better questions until the thesis becomes clear enough to act on, reject, or monitor.
