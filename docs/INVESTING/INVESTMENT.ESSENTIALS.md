# Investment Essentials

Investing has its own language. At first, a sentence like "this stock looks expensive because its P/E ratio is high" can feel like a foreign language. Once the basic terms are clear, the sentence becomes easier: a share is ownership in a company, earnings are profit, and the P/E ratio compares the share price to the company's earnings.

Good investors do not need to know every finance term immediately. They need enough understanding to read business performance, compare similar companies, and avoid being fooled by a single attractive number.

## Accounting in a Nutshell

Financial statements are the business record of a company. They are not perfect, but they are the best starting point for understanding how a company makes money, spends money, owns assets, owes debts, and generates cash.

Public companies usually provide three core statements:

- Income statement: how much money the business made and spent.
- Balance sheet: what the business owns and owes.
- Cash flow statement: how much cash moved through the business.

Accounting standards exist because investors need companies to report numbers in a consistent way. Without standards, comparing companies would be chaotic.

Context matters. A number that looks good in one industry may be weak in another. A number that looks bad may be reasonable if it supports a deliberate strategy.

## Income Statement

An income statement shows performance over a period, usually a quarter or year.

The main parts are:

- Revenue: money earned from sales or services.
- Expenses: costs required to earn that revenue.
- Net income: profit after expenses.

The simplest idea is:

$\large{\text{Net income} = \text{Revenue} - \text{Expenses}}$

Investors compare results over time. For many businesses, comparing a quarter with the same quarter in a previous year is more useful than comparing it with the previous quarter, because business activity can be seasonal.

The time value of money also matters. A dollar today is worth more than a dollar later because it can be invested, and because inflation can reduce buying power. If a company earns the same net income for 10 years without growth, the real value of that income may decline.

> Example: A consulting firm earns revenue by selling services. Its service costs are mostly salaries, and its research spending includes training, certifications, and new skill development.

[Image placeholder: Consulting company revenue-to-expenses model with R&D, services, and other expenses. Source: `Investing.for.Programmers-2025-2.pdf`, Fig. 2.2.]

If research expenses increase sharply, the reason matters.

- Bullish view: the company may be preparing for new projects or new services.
- Bearish view: the company may need outside capital because its business is under pressure.

[Image placeholder: Consulting company model where R&D rises and extra expenses are funded with outside capital. Source: `Investing.for.Programmers-2025-2.pdf`, Fig. 2.3.]

If research and service costs fall while revenue stays stable, that can also tell two stories.

- Bullish view: the company may have become more efficient.
- Bearish view: the company may be cutting growth investment.

[Image placeholder: Consulting company model where research and service spending fall and surplus rises. Source: `Investing.for.Programmers-2025-2.pdf`, Fig. 2.4.]

The lesson is simple: accounting numbers create questions. They do not answer everything by themselves.

## Balance Sheet

The balance sheet shows what a company owns and owes.

$\large{\text{Assets} = \text{Liabilities} + \text{Shareholders' equity}}$

- Assets: what the company owns.
- Liabilities: what the company owes.
- Shareholders' equity: what remains for owners after debts are paid.

If liabilities are greater than assets, the company may be in trouble.

Liquidity is how easily an asset can be turned into cash. Cash is highly liquid. A factory, specialized machinery, or rare property may be valuable but hard to sell quickly.

Capital expenses are long-term investments in assets. Operating expenses are regular costs needed to run the business. Some businesses need large physical assets. Others can operate with fewer assets but higher recurring service costs. Neither model is automatically better; the investor must understand the business.

## Free Cash Flow

Free cash flow is the cash left after the company pays for operations and capital investments.

$\large{\text{Free cash flow} = \text{Operating cash flow} - \text{Capital expenditures}}$

Free cash flow matters because bills are paid with cash, not with accounting promises.

> Example: Eugene owns an antique car business. His inventory may be worth millions, but if he has no cash, he may still struggle to pay wages or repairs. He cannot easily pay the plumber with a rare car or with money a customer still owes him.

A profitable-looking business can still become insolvent if cash is trapped in inventory, receivables, or assets that cannot be sold quickly.

## Industry Classification

Companies should be compared with similar companies. A supermarket, a software company, a utility, and a bank do not make money in the same way.

[Image placeholder: Sector classification hierarchy showing sectors, industry groups, industries, and subindustries. Source: `Investing.for.Programmers-2025-2.pdf`, Fig. 2.5.]

Industry classification helps investors understand:

- How a company makes money.
- Which ratios matter most.
- Which risks are normal for the sector.
- Which peers are fair comparisons.
- Which external forces may affect the business.

## Sectors and Economic Cycles

Economic cycles affect sectors differently. During a downturn, people may delay buying cars, electronics, or vacations. They still need food, electricity, medicine, and basic household products.

This sample from the 2008-2010 crisis shows why business type matters. Ford fell much more severely than Walmart because cars are easier to delay than groceries.

The economic cycle has four broad stages:

- Expansion: jobs and spending rise.
- Peak: growth reaches a high point.
- Contraction: spending and confidence fall.
- Trough: the downturn bottoms before recovery.

Cyclical companies are strongly affected by the cycle. Defensive companies tend to remain steadier because they sell essentials.

## Capitalization

Market capitalization is the market value of a public company.

$\large{\text{Market capitalization} = \text{share price} \times \text{shares outstanding}}$

Common size categories:

- Mega-cap: $200 billion or more.
- Large-cap: $10 billion to $200 billion.
- Mid-cap: $2 billion to $10 billion.
- Small-cap: $250 million to $2 billion.
- Micro-cap: less than $250 million.

Large companies are often safer, but they may have less room to grow. Small companies can grow dramatically, but they can also fail. A company is not safe forever just because it is large today.

Intrinsic value is an estimate of what an asset is truly worth. If the estimated intrinsic value is far above market value, the investor may have found an opportunity. But intrinsic value is always an estimate, not certainty.

## Metrics and Ratios

A metric is one measurement. A ratio compares two measurements.

- Metric example: revenue.
- Ratio example: price-to-earnings, which compares share price to earnings per share.

Ratios are useful because they make companies easier to compare. But a single ratio should never carry the whole decision. The investor should compare several ratios, understand the industry, and ask whether the numbers support the business story.

| Company | Market cap | Sales | Employees | P/E | Beta |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| NVIDIA | 3457.97B | 148.51B | 36,000 | 45.65 | 2.12 |
| Walmart | 779.85B | 685.09B | 2,100,000 | 41.81 | 0.69 |

This comparison shows why context matters. Walmart has far more sales and employees. NVIDIA has a much higher market value and beta. The businesses are different, so the numbers need interpretation.

Beta measures how volatile a stock is compared with the overall market. A beta above 1 means the stock tends to move more than the market. A beta below 1 means it tends to move less.

## Liquidity

Liquidity ratios ask whether a company can pay short-term bills.

Current ratio:

$\large{\text{Current ratio} = \frac{\text{current assets}}{\text{current liabilities}}}$

Quick ratio:

$\large{\text{Quick ratio} = \frac{\text{cash + marketable securities + receivables}}{\text{current liabilities}}}$

The quick ratio is stricter because it excludes inventory.

| NVIDIA (NVDA) | Technology | Semiconductors | 3.388 | 2.857 |
| Salesforce (CRM) | Technology | Software - Application | 1.069 | 0.899 |

Different sectors naturally carry different liquidity profiles. A supermarket must hold inventory. A software company may not.

## Debt

Debt is money the company owes, especially obligations that require interest payments.

Debt-to-equity ratio:

$\large{\text{Debt-to-equity} = \frac{\text{total liabilities}}{\text{shareholders' equity}}}$

| Company | Sector | Industry | Debt-to-equity |
| --- | --- | --- | ---: |
| Apple (AAPL) | Technology | Consumer Electronics | 146.994 |
| Walmart (WMT) | Consumer Defensive | Discount Stores | 74.138 |
| NVIDIA (NVDA) | Technology | Semiconductors | 12.267 |

A high debt-to-equity ratio is not automatically bad. It may reflect danger, or it may reflect a deliberate strategy if the company can borrow cheaply and invest profitably.

Interest coverage is also important:

$\large{\text{Interest coverage} = \frac{\text{EBIT}}{\text{interest expense}}}$

If interest coverage is below 1, the company is not earning enough to cover interest from current profits. That is a serious warning.

## Earnings

Earnings per share shows how much profit belongs to each ordinary share.

$\large{\text{EPS} = \frac{\text{profit} - \text{preferred dividends}}{\text{shares outstanding}}}$

Earnings calls can move stock prices because investors compare reported earnings with expectations. If a company beats expectations, the stock may rise. If it disappoints, the stock may fall.

Free cash flow per share shows how much free cash flow belongs to each share:

$\large{\text{Free cash flow per share} = \frac{\text{free cash flow}}{\text{shares outstanding}}}$

## Valuation

Valuation asks whether the price is reasonable.

For public companies, market capitalization is easy to calculate. For private companies and start-ups, valuation can be much less certain because financial history may be limited and assumptions may be optimistic.

Price-to-earnings ratio:

$\large{\text{P/E} = \frac{\text{share price}}{\text{earnings per share}}}$

A high P/E may mean the company is expensive. It may also mean investors expect strong growth. That is why P/E should be compared with peers, sector averages, and growth expectations.

Price/earnings-to-growth ratio:

$\large{\text{PEG} = \frac{\text{P/E}}{\text{earnings growth}}}$

> Example: One investor may say NVIDIA is overvalued because its P/E is higher than the sector median. Another may say it is still attractive because growth makes the PEG ratio stronger. The same data can support different stories, so the investor must explain which story is more convincing.

Other valuation ratios:

- Price-to-sales: price paid for each dollar of sales.
- Price-to-book: price paid for each dollar of equity.

## Profitability

Profitability asks how well the company turns resources into profit.

Return on assets:

$\large{\text{ROA} = \frac{\text{net income}}{\text{total assets}}}$

Return on equity:

$\large{\text{ROE} = \frac{\text{net income}}{\text{shareholders' equity}}}$

Profit margin:

$\large{\text{Profit margin} = \frac{\text{net income}}{\text{revenue}}}$

Profitability differs by industry. A supermarket may sell huge volumes with thin margins. A software or semiconductor company may sell less volume but earn higher margins.

## Dividends

Dividends return cash to shareholders. Companies often pay dividends when they are mature and have fewer high-return growth opportunities.

Dividend yield:

$\large{\text{Dividend yield} = \frac{\text{annual dividend per share}}{\text{share price}} \times 100}$

Payout ratio:

$\large{\text{Payout ratio} = \frac{\text{dividends paid}}{\text{net income}} \times 100}$

Dividend investors should watch:

- Yield.
- Payout ratio.
- Dividend growth.
- Payment history.
- Payment schedule.
- Tax treatment.
- Whether the dividend is supported by cash flow.

> Example: A dividend is usually deducted from the share price on the payment date. If the investor also pays withholding tax, the cash received may be less valuable than expected. This is why dividends and bond coupons are not the same.

Stock buybacks are another way to reward shareholders. When a company buys back shares, it reduces the share count. Fewer shares can make each remaining share represent a larger ownership claim.

## Ownership

Ownership structure changes over time.

- Outstanding shares: all shares held by shareholders.
- Float: shares available for public trading after excluding insider and large locked-up holdings.

Companies can change ownership economics by:

- Issuing new shares: increases supply and dilutes existing owners.
- Repurchasing shares: reduces supply and may increase each remaining share's claim.

Insider transactions can also matter. Executives buying shares may suggest confidence. Executives selling shares may be a warning, but it can also be normal wealth management.

Stock-based compensation can create dilution. It can help a company attract employees, especially when cash is limited, but it increases the number of shares over time. More shares mean each existing share owns a smaller slice of the company.

## Sustainability

Some investors care not only about profit but also about what their money supports. ESG analysis looks at environmental, social, and governance issues.

Sustainability analysis may include:

- Environmental impact.
- Labor practices.
- Governance quality.
- Involvement in controversial industries.
- Peer comparison.
- Company sustainability reports.

Lower ESG risk scores are often better when the scale measures risk.

## External Assessments

External ratings and target prices can help, but they should be treated as opinions, not truth.

Common external signals:

- Analyst ratings: buy, hold, sell, or similar recommendations.
- Quantitative ratings: calculated scores based on selected factors.
- Crowdsourced ratings: opinions from a wider investor community.
- Target prices: estimates of future share price.
- Upgrades and downgrades: changes in analyst opinion over time.

Experts can be wrong. Ratings were part of the problem before the 2008 mortgage crisis, when risky assets received favorable assessments. Investors should use external assessments as inputs, not commands.

> Example: A newspaper may predict that stocks will fall, but the market may rise anyway. Good investing is not about being perfectly right. It is about making decisions that work across uncertainty.

The key principle is probability. Investors cannot know the future. They manage uncertainty through research, comparison, diversification, and discipline.
