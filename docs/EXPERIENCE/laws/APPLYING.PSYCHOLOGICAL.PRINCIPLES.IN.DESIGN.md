# Chapter 11: Applying Psychological Principles in Design

Behavioral and cognitive psychology give designers a practical foundation for creating human-centered experiences. Just as architects design better buildings when they understand how people experience space, designers create better products when they understand how people perceive, decide, remember, and act.

The challenge is turning that understanding into part of the design process. Teams need ways to internalize psychological principles, discuss them together, and translate them into design principles that reflect the goals and priorities of the organization.

## Building Awareness

Building awareness is one of the simplest and most effective ways for designers to internalize psychological principles. The goal is to make the principles visible, discussable, and familiar enough that they naturally influence design decisions.

### Visibility

The easiest way to internalize the principles is to make them visible in the team's working space.

One practical example is printing Laws of UX posters and placing them where the team can see them regularly. The posters serve as reminders of the psychological principles that can guide decisions during the design process. They also remind teams how people perceive and process information.

### Show-and-Tell

Show-and-tell is another useful way to build awareness. The format is simple: someone shows an example, tool, technique, finding, or idea to the team and explains why it matters.

Design teams benefit from regular knowledge-sharing time. It spreads useful information at low cost and in a format people are likely to remember. Topics might include design techniques, new tools, usability test findings, project recaps, and psychological principles.

Show-and-tell also helps team members build speaking confidence and establish subject matter expertise. More broadly, it signals that the organization values continual learning, dialogue, and shared knowledge.

Awareness alone may not fully embed psychological principles into the design process, but it can influence decisions. To make the principles more central to team decision-making, teams can connect them to explicit design principles.

## Design Principles

As design teams grow, the number of design decisions increases. If every major decision depends on design leadership, leaders can become bottlenecks. Work slows down, and the team becomes less scalable.

The opposite problem can also occur: individuals make decisions independently without a shared standard for quality, objectives, or design vision. When that happens, "good design" becomes a moving target. Output becomes inconsistent because each person may be using a different definition.

Design principles help solve this. They are guidelines that express the priorities and goals of a design team. They provide a foundation for reasoning and decision-making.

Strong design principles help teams understand what they value, how they approach problems, and what successful design looks like. They act as a North Star. Decisions become faster, more consistent, and less dependent on gatekeepers.

When design principles are done well, they can shape not only a team but the broader organization.

## Defining Your Principles

Teams can define design principles in many ways. A common approach is to run a workshop or series of workshop activities.

### Identify the Team

Start by identifying who should participate. A good approach is to invite the people whose work will be affected by the principles. It can also help to include design leadership and stakeholders outside the immediate team because they bring useful perspective.

The more people involved in shaping the principles, the easier adoption becomes later.

### Align and Define

Once the group is identified, align on the purpose of the exercise. Make sure everyone understands what design principles are and how they will be used.

The team should also define success criteria. For example, what does each principle need to do to be valuable? What kinds of decisions should it help resolve?

### Diverge

Next, generate ideas. Ask each participant to brainstorm design principles for a set amount of time, such as 10-15 minutes. Each idea should be written separately, often on its own sticky note.

By the end of this step, each participant should have a set of possible principles.

### Converge

After idea generation, bring the ideas together and identify themes. Participants share their ideas, and a facilitator helps organize them into related groups.

Once the themes are visible, participants vote on the ones that feel most appropriate for the team and organization. Dot voting is a common method: each participant gets a limited number of dots, usually 5-10, and uses them to vote on the themes they value most.

### Refine and Apply

Next, refine the themes into clear principles. Consolidate overlapping ideas and rewrite them so they are direct, memorable, and actionable.

Then identify where and how the principles should be applied. The goal is to connect the principles to actual team rituals, reviews, documentation, product decisions, and organizational habits.

### Circulate and Advocate

Finally, share the principles widely and advocate for adoption. They can appear in posters, desktop wallpapers, notebooks, shared documentation, and team spaces.

The goal is to make the principles visible and easy to reference. People who participated in the workshop should also advocate for the principles inside and outside the immediate team.

## Best Practices

Design principles are useful only if they help teams make decisions. A few practices make them stronger.

### Good Design Principles Are Not Truisms

Good principles are direct, clear, and actionable. They should not be bland statements that everyone already agrees with.

"Design should be intuitive" is too vague. It does not help the team make a trade-off or choose between options.

### Good Design Principles Solve Real Questions

Principles should help resolve real design questions and drive decisions. They should be specific enough to guide action, but not so narrow that they apply only to one scenario.

### Good Design Principles Are Opinionated

Useful principles include a point of view. They express priorities and help the team say no when needed.

### Good Design Principles Are Memorable

Principles that are hard to remember are less likely to be used. They should feel relevant to the team's needs and ambitions, and to the organization as a whole.

## Connecting Principles to Laws

Once a team has design principles, each principle can be connected to the psychological laws discussed in this book. This connection explains why the principle matters and grounds it in human behavior.

For example, a design principle might be "clarity over abundance of choice." This principle is useful because it names a priority and a trade-off. To connect it to a psychological law, identify the law that best supports the goal of clarity.

Hick's law is a good match because it states that decision time increases with the number and complexity of available choices.

After connecting a design principle to a law, the team can define rules that make the principle more concrete. Rules provide constraints that guide design decisions in a more prescriptive way.

For "clarity over abundance of choice," possible rules might include:

- Limit choices to no more than three items at a time.
- Provide brief explanations when useful, keeping them clear and under 80 characters.

These are examples, not universal rules. Each team should define rules that fit its product, users, and organization.

_**Figure 11-2.** example design principle, observation, and rules._

Figure 11-2 shows a framework made of a goal, a psychological observation, and design rules. The design principle defines the goal. The law explains the human behavior behind it. The rules guide day-to-day decisions.

Another example is "familiarity over novelty." This principle is direct, opinionated, and useful because it names a trade-off.

Jakob's law is a strong match. It states that users spend most of their time on other sites and expect your site to work like the ones they already know.

Rules connected to this principle might include:

- Use common design patterns to reinforce familiarity with the interface.
- Avoid distracting users with flashy UI or quirky animations.
