# Chapter 7: Aesthetic-Usability Effect

_People often perceive aesthetically pleasing designs as more usable._

## Overview

Design is not only about how something looks. It is also about how it works. But visual appeal still matters because aesthetics can influence how usable a product feels.

An attractive interface can create a positive emotional response, improve perceived credibility, and make people believe the product works better. People form a fast first impression when they see a design, and that first impression affects how they interpret the rest of the experience.

This phenomenon is called the aesthetic-usability effect. It means that people tend to associate beauty with usability, even when the connection is not fully supported by the actual interaction.

## Origins

The aesthetic-usability effect traces back to a 1995 study by Masaaki Kurosu and Kaori Kashimura from the Hitachi Design Center. At the time, the relationship between aesthetics and digital interfaces had not been studied deeply.

Kurosu and Kashimura investigated the relationship between inherent usability and what they called apparent usability. They tested 26 ATM interface layout patterns with 252 participants. Each participant rated each design for both functionality and aesthetics.

Participants used a 10-point rating scale to evaluate usability and visual attractiveness. The results showed that perceived usability was strongly influenced by perceived attractiveness.

Later research supported the finding. The 2000 study "What Is Beautiful Is Usable" by Noam Tractinsky and colleagues further confirmed that interface aesthetics affect people's perception of system usability. Other research has explored how attractiveness affects trust, credibility, and usability testing.

## Psychology Concept: Automatic Cognitive Processing

People really do judge books by their covers. That is not always bad. Fast judgment is necessary because carefully analyzing every object around us would be inefficient and sometimes dangerous.

Automatic cognitive processing lets us react quickly. Before we consciously focus on what we are seeing, we begin forming an opinion based on past experience.

Daniel Kahneman describes this relationship in *Thinking, Fast and Slow* through two modes of thought: System 1 and System 2.

System 1 is fast, automatic, and effortless. It operates with little or no voluntary control. It helps us recognize objects, detect danger, direct attention, avoid loss, and respond quickly based on experience or practice. System 1 generates intuitions, feelings, intentions, and impressions for System 2.

System 2 is slower and more deliberate. It requires mental effort and attention. It takes over when System 1 encounters difficulty and more detailed processing is needed. Complex problem-solving, focused research, memory search, advanced mathematical operations, and situational awareness all rely on System 2.

The two systems work together to minimize effort and optimize performance. System 1 handles most everyday thinking and action. System 2 steps in when necessary.

This matters deeply for digital products. Users rely on System 1 to quickly scan an interface, identify information that seems relevant, and ignore what does not appear useful. Anything that does not seem connected to the user's goal is often passed over.

The aesthetic-usability effect starts in this fast, first-impression layer. Research suggests that people can form an opinion about a website within 50 milliseconds, and visual appeal is a major factor. That initial visceral response often remains stable even after users spend more time with the site.

## Examples

Braun is a classic example of aesthetics and usability working together. The German electronics company, especially under the design direction of Dieter Rams, shaped generations of product designers through a balance of functional minimalism and visual beauty.

Rams's "less but better" approach emphasized that form should follow function. The result was a body of products that felt both practical and beautiful.

The Braun SK4 record player is one example. It was nicknamed "Snow White's Coffin" because of its white metal casing and transparent lid.

![Braun SK4 record player designed by Hans Gugelot and Dieter Rams.](/.attachments/7-3-laws-braun.record.player.png)

_**Figure 7-3.** Braun SK4 record player designed by Hans Gugelot and Dieter Rams._

Figure 7-3 shows the Braun SK4. Its powder-coated sheet metal, elmwood side panels, and plexiglass cover stood apart from the ornamented all-wood products common at the time. The transparent cover also solved a functional problem by avoiding the rattling that occurred with metal covers at higher volumes.

The SK4 marked a shift in design history. Electronic devices no longer needed to disguise themselves as furniture. They could be useful, beautiful objects in their own right.

Apple continues much of Braun's legacy: functional minimalism balanced with refined aesthetics. Products such as the iPod, iPhone, and iMac echo Braun's clean industrial design while emphasizing ease of use.

## Technique: Usability Testing

Designers are not the user. Teams are usually too close to their own work to see it objectively or predict exactly how real users will interact with it.

Usability testing helps teams observe real people using a product, prototype, or design. The goal is to uncover problems, discover opportunities, and learn about user behaviors and preferences by asking participants to perform realistic tasks.

This method is one of the most effective ways to understand how well a design will perform in the hands of real users. The findings can guide future iterations.

### Planning the Test

Start by defining the objectives of the test. Clarify what the team wants to learn. For example, the goal might be to understand how well users complete a specific workflow or how clearly they understand a feature.

Next, write a test script based on tasks you want participants to perform. These tasks act as prompts that let you observe real behavior.

Finally, recruit representative users. Do not rely only on feedback from your own team or company unless the product is intended exclusively for them.

### Conducting the Test

Ask participants to perform realistic tasks using a prototype or actual product. Tasks may be specific or open-ended depending on the research goal.

Remain neutral. Make sure participants understand that the design is being tested, not them. Avoid priming them with exact words from the interface.

During the test, listen carefully and avoid biasing the participant. It is common to ask people to think aloud so you can understand their goals, thoughts, and motivations.

Measure both what participants say and what they do. A participant's subjective rating does not always match how easily or quickly they completed a task.

Observation itself can influence behavior. This is known as observer bias. To reduce it, remind participants that honest feedback is helpful, that the goal is to improve the design, and that their performance is not being judged.

### Synthesizing the Data

After the test, synthesize the data into insights that can guide action. This is best done as a group activity with the people who observed or participated in the research. Including the broader project team can build shared understanding and empathy.

Start with the goals of the study and how it was conducted. Document who participated, who helped run the study, and how data was gathered.

Then collect quotes and observations related to user goals, priorities, actions, motivations, pain points, habits, interactions, tools, and context. Group those observations by themes or patterns.

Focus on insights before jumping to solutions. Look back at the original research questions and ask whether the observed patterns help answer them. Avoid identifying big patterns too early, and distinguish direct observations from interpretations.

Finally, document the synthesis in a shareable format. Keep it concise, but include research goals, methods, insights, and recommendations. Support insights with specific examples from the research.

Sometimes research reveals what the team needs to learn next. That is a valid outcome and can become the starting point for future research.

## Key Consideration: Effect on Usability Tests

The aesthetic-usability effect has a significant caveat. Because people often believe attractive designs work better, they may be more forgiving of usability problems.

Psychologists Andreas Sonderegger and Jürgen Sauer studied this effect in usability testing. They used a computer simulation of a mobile phone and asked 60 adolescents to complete common tasks. The two simulations were functionally identical but visually different: one was attractive and the other was intentionally unattractive.

![Two mobile phone prototypes used in Sonderegger and Sauer's experiment.](/.attachments/7-6-laws-two.prototypes.png)

_**Figure 7-6.** Two mobile phone prototypes used in Sonderegger and Sauer's experiment._

Figure 7-6 shows the two prototypes used in the experiment.

Participants rated the more attractive phone as more usable. The researchers also found that the attractive version improved performance, reducing task completion times.

This means visual quality can mask usability issues, even when the product is not actually easier to use. That matters during usability testing, where finding issues is the whole point.

To reduce this effect, researchers should listen to what users say and pay even closer attention to what they do. Questions should guide participants beyond visual appearance so the team can uncover usability issues that aesthetics might otherwise hide.
