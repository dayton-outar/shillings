## A Real-World Example

Imagine the Board of Directors at ABC Insurance Company reviewing last year’s results. One board member notes how auto insurance dragged down the whole company — they lost money on every dollar of auto premiums but made a small profit on other lines. Investment income helped turn the overall result into a small profit. The president emphasizes that fixing auto insurance profitability is the top priority this year.

This chapter explains how insurance companies track their money through balance sheets, income statements, profitability measures, and how they set prices (rates) for policies.

## Property and Casualty Insurers

To understand an insurance company’s finances, we look at two key reports: the **balance sheet** (a snapshot of what the company owns and owes on a specific date) and the **income and expense statement** (shows money coming in and going out over a period).

### Balance Sheet

A balance sheet follows this simple equation:

${ \text{Total Assets} = \text{Total Liabilities} + \text{Owners' Equity (Policyholders' Surplus)} }$

**Assets**  
These are what the company owns. Insurance companies invest the premiums they collect, so most assets are financial investments like bonds, stocks, real estate, and cash. For ABC Insurance, total assets were $440 million, with bonds being the biggest part.

**Liabilities**  
These are what the company owes. The two biggest items are:

- **Loss Reserves**: Money set aside to pay claims that have already happened but not yet been fully paid. This includes reported claims still being processed and claims that have occurred but haven’t been reported yet (called IBNR). ABC had $120 million in loss reserves.
- **Unearned Premium Reserve**: Money collected for future coverage that hasn’t been “earned” yet. If a customer pays for a full year upfront, only part of that money is earned each month. ABC had $101 million here.

Other liabilities include expenses to settle claims, commissions owed to agents, and taxes.

**Policyholders’ Surplus**  
This is the cushion — basically Assets minus Liabilities. It shows how much financial strength the company has if claims turn out higher than expected. ABC’s surplus was $185 million.

### Income and Expense Statement

This report shows revenues and expenses over a year.

**Revenues**  
Main sources:
- Premiums earned (the part of premiums that covers the time the policy was actually in force).
- Investment income (interest from bonds, dividends from stocks, rental income, gains from selling investments).

**Expenses**  
Main costs:
- Claims paid and loss-adjustment expenses (costs to investigate and settle claims).
- Underwriting expenses (commissions to agents, taxes, general operating costs).

For ABC, total revenues were $223 million and total expenses were about $212 million, leaving a net income of $7.5 million after taxes.

## Measuring Profit or Loss

Insurers track performance in several ways:

- **Loss Ratio**: Compares claims and adjustment costs to premiums earned.  
  ${ \text{Loss Ratio} = \frac{\text{Incurred Losses + Loss Adjustment Expenses}}{\text{Premiums Earned}} }$  
  ABC’s loss ratio was 0.720 (or 72%).

- **Expense Ratio**: Underwriting expenses divided by premiums written.  
  ${ \text{Expense Ratio} = \frac{\text{Underwriting Expenses}}{\text{Premiums Written}} }$  
  ABC’s was 0.314 (or 31.4%).

- **Combined Ratio**: Loss ratio + Expense ratio.  
  ABC’s was 1.034 (or 103.4%). Over 100% means an underwriting loss.

- **Investment Income Ratio**: Investment income compared to earned premiums.

- **Overall Operating Ratio**: Combined ratio minus investment income ratio. Below 100% means the company is profitable overall. ABC’s was 94.6%.

Even if underwriting loses money, strong investment income can still make the company profitable.

The property and casualty industry has often struggled with profitability, with combined ratios frequently above 100% in many years.

> The property and casualty insurance industry lags most other industries in profitability... The property and casualty insurance industry has not been highly profitable over time.

## Life Insurance Companies

Life insurance finances are similar but have some important differences due to long-term policies.

### Balance Sheet

**Assets**  
Life insurers also invest heavily, but they focus more on long-term investments (bonds, mortgages, real estate) to match their long-term promises. They also have “policy loans” — money borrowed by policyholders against their cash value. Some assets are in separate accounts for variable products.

**Liabilities**  
The biggest item is **policy reserves** — money set aside to pay future claims and benefits. These come from the extra premiums collected in early years of cash-value policies.

Other reserves include amounts held for future payouts to beneficiaries and an asset valuation reserve to handle investment fluctuations.

**Policyholders’ Surplus**  
Similar to property/casualty, but generally more stable because life insurance has less risk of sudden big losses.

### Income and Expense Statement

**Revenues**: Premiums from life, annuity, and health policies + investment income.  
**Expenses**: Death benefits, annuity payments, surrender benefits, commissions, taxes, and operating costs.

Profitability can be measured by net income compared to assets or return on surplus. Life insurers have generally shown steadier returns than property and casualty companies.

## Rate Making in Property and Casualty Insurance

Setting prices (rates) is critical. Rates must be fair, adequate, and not too high.

### Objectives in Rate Making

**Regulatory Objectives** (required by law):
- Rates must be **adequate** (enough to pay claims and expenses).
- Rates must **not be excessive** (not unfairly high).
- Rates must **not be unfairly discriminatory** (similar risks pay similar rates).

**Business Objectives**:
- Simple and easy to use.
- Stable in the short term but responsive to changes over time.
- Encourage loss prevention (e.g., discounts for safety features).

### Basic Rate-Making Definitions

- **Rate**: Price per unit of insurance.
- **Exposure Unit**: The thing being insured (e.g., one car for a year, $100 of property coverage).
- **Pure Premium**: Amount needed just for losses and claim-adjustment costs.
- **Loading**: Extra amount added for expenses, profit, and safety margin.
- **Gross Premium**: What the customer actually pays.

### Rate-Making Methods

1. **Judgment Rating**  
   The underwriter looks at each unique risk and sets the rate based on experience and judgment. Used for very different or unusual risks, like ocean marine insurance.

2. **Class Rating**  
   Similar risks are grouped together and charged the same rate. This is the most common method for personal insurance like auto and homeowners.  
   Two main ways to calculate:
   - **Pure Premium Method**: Calculate average losses per exposure unit, then add loading.  
     ${ \text{Gross Rate} = \frac{\text{Pure Premium}}{1 - \text{Expense Ratio}} }$
   - **Loss Ratio Method**: Compare actual losses to expected losses and adjust rates up or down.

3. **Merit Rating**  
   Starts with a class rate but adjusts it based on the individual customer’s situation or history.

   - **Schedule Rating**: Adjusts for specific physical features (construction, fire protection, etc.).
   - **Experience Rating**: Adjusts based on the customer’s past loss history.
   - **Retrospective Rating**: Final premium is calculated after the policy period based on actual losses.
