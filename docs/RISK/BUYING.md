# Chapter 13: Buying Life Insurance

When you buy life insurance, comparing just the first-year premium doesn’t tell the full story. You need to understand the long-term cost.

## Determining the Cost of Life Insurance

The true cost of life insurance is what you pay in premiums minus what you eventually get back (cash value and dividends), considering the time value of money. Four key factors matter: annual premiums, cash values, dividends, and interest you could have earned elsewhere.

### Traditional Net Cost Method

This older method simply adds up premiums over a period (like 20 years), subtracts expected dividends and the cash value at the end, then divides by the number of years.

**Example results often looked too good** — sometimes even showing “negative cost,” which suggested insurance was free. That’s unrealistic and misleading.

**Main problem**: It ignores the time value of money. Money you pay today could have earned interest if invested elsewhere.

### Interest-Adjusted Cost Method

This better approach accounts for interest by growing premiums and dividends at a reasonable rate (usually 5%).

There are two main versions:

**Surrender Cost Index**  
Useful if you might cash out the policy after 10 or 20 years. It shows the cost assuming you surrender (cancel) the policy and take the cash value.

**Net Payment Cost Index**  
Useful if you plan to keep the policy until you die. It ignores cash value and focuses on death benefit protection.

### Using Interest-Adjusted Cost Data

- Compare only similar policies (same type and benefits).  
- Shop for the policy, not just the company.  
- Small differences in numbers often don’t matter much.  
- These indexes work best for new policies, not for deciding whether to replace an old one.  
- Choose the policy type that fits your needs first, then compare costs.

## NAIC Policy Illustration Model Regulation

Most states require insurers to provide clear illustrations showing how policies work. These include guaranteed and non-guaranteed values, using current, midpoint, and guaranteed interest rates. Illustrations must be signed and explain that future values can change.

> **Be Careful in Replacing an Existing Life Insurance Policy**  
> Think twice before swapping policies. Consider new medical underwriting, surrender charges, new policy fees, tax consequences, and the fact that new policies have contestability and suicide clauses that restart the two-year clocks. Do a full cost-benefit analysis.

## Rate of Return on Saving Component

Cash-value policies combine insurance protection with a savings element. Knowing the return on the savings part is important.

### Linton Yield

This method estimates the average annual return if you keep the policy for many years. Returns are often negative in early years due to high upfront costs and commissions. Most experts recommend holding cash-value policies at least 20 years for decent returns.

### Yearly Rate-of-Return Method

Professor Joseph Belth created a simpler way to calculate the yearly return on your savings:

$\large{
i = \frac{(CV + D) + (YPT)(DB - CV)(0.001)}{(P + CVP)} - 1
}$

Where:  
- $i$ = yearly rate of return  
- $CV$ = cash value at end of year  
- $D$ = dividend for the year  
- $YPT$ = assumed price per $1,000 of protection (benchmark)  
- $DB$ = death benefit  
- $P$ = annual premium  
- $CVP$ = cash value at end of previous year

## Taxation of Life Insurance

### Federal Income Tax

- Death benefits paid as a lump sum are generally income-tax free.  
- Premiums are not tax-deductible.  
- Dividends are usually tax-free until they exceed your total premiums paid.  
- Cash value grows tax-deferred. If you surrender the policy, gains (cash value above premiums paid minus dividends) are taxed as ordinary income.

### Federal Estate Tax

If you own or control the policy at death (e.g., can change beneficiaries or borrow cash value), the full death benefit is included in your estate.

## Shopping for Life Insurance

Follow these seven steps:

**Determine Whether You Need Life Insurance**  
Single people with no dependents usually need little or none (maybe just burial coverage). People with dependents, mortgages, or estate liquidity needs often require substantial coverage.

**Estimate the Amount of Life Insurance You Need**  
Use the needs approach: look at your family’s ongoing expenses, debts, future goals, Social Security survivor benefits, and existing assets.

**Decide on the Best Type of Life Insurance for You**  
- Temporary need or tight budget → Term insurance.  
- Lifetime protection or forced savings → Consider whole life or similar cash-value policies.  
Only buy what you can comfortably afford long-term.

**Decide Whether You Want a Policy That Pays Dividends**  
Participating policies (often from mutual companies) refund part of the premium as dividends when the company does well. They have historically performed better for long-term policies.

**Shop Around for a Policy That Provides Good Value**  
Big cost differences exist. Always compare interest-adjusted costs. For term insurance you might convert later, buy from a company strong in permanent coverage too.

**Consider the Financial Strength of the Insurer**  
Check ratings from A.M. Best, Fitch, Moody’s, or S&P. Even with state guaranty funds, weak companies can cause delays and problems.
